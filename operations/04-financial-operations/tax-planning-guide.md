# Tax Planning Guide

## Denvan Holdings LLC - Texas LLC Tax Strategy

---

## Texas Tax Advantages

Texas has **no state income tax** -- one of the biggest advantages of being headquartered here. However, there are still tax obligations:

### Texas Franchise Tax (Margin Tax)
- **What:** Texas's version of a business tax, based on revenue margins
- **Rate:** 0.375% (wholesale/retail) or 0.75% (other businesses)
- **Exemption:** No tax due if total revenue is under **$2.47 million** (2024 threshold)
- **Filing:** Annual report due **May 15** each year
- **Where:** Texas Comptroller of Public Accounts
- **Note:** You must file the report even if no tax is due

### Federal Tax Obligations

As a single-member LLC, Denvan Holdings is a **disregarded entity** for federal tax purposes. All income flows through to the owner's personal tax return (Form 1040, Schedule C).

| Tax | Rate | When | How |
|-----|------|------|-----|
| Federal Income Tax | 10-37% (marginal) | April 15 annually | Form 1040 + Schedule C |
| Self-Employment Tax | 15.3% (on net earnings) | April 15 annually | Schedule SE |
| Estimated Tax Payments | Based on projected liability | Quarterly (Apr 15, Jun 15, Sep 15, Jan 15) | EFTPS or IRS Direct Pay |

## Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

If you expect to owe $1,000+ in taxes, you must make quarterly estimated payments.

### How to Calculate

1. Estimate annual net profit from all subsidiaries + parent
2. Calculate self-employment tax: Net profit x 92.35% x 15.3%
3. Calculate income tax: Net profit x your marginal tax rate
4. Divide total by 4 = quarterly payment amount

### Example: $200,000 Net Annual Profit

| Tax | Calculation | Amount |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| Self-Employment Tax | $200,000 x 0.9235 x 0.153 | $28,260 |
| Federal Income Tax (est. 24% bracket) | $200,000 x 0.24 | $48,000 |
| **Total Estimated Tax** | | **$76,260** |
| **Quarterly Payment** | | **$19,065** |

### Payment Schedule

| Quarter | Period | Due Date |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Q1 | Jan 1 - Mar 31 | April 15 |
| Q2 | Apr 1 - May 31 | June 15 |
| Q3 | Jun 1 - Aug 31 | September 15 |
| Q4 | Sep 1 - Dec 31 | January 15 (next year) |

**Pay via:** EFTPS.gov (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System)

## Deductible Business Expenses

Everything on this list can reduce your taxable income:

### Fully Deductible
- Office rent and utilities
- Employee salaries and payroll taxes
- Contractor payments (1099)
- Business insurance premiums
- Software subscriptions and technology costs
- Professional services (legal, accounting, consulting)
- Marketing and advertising expenses
- Business travel (transportation, lodging)
- Business phone and internet
- Office supplies and equipment (under $2,500 per item)
- Continuing education and professional development
- Bank fees and payment processing fees
- Business license and permit fees
- DBA registration fees

### Partially Deductible
- Meals (50% deductible if business-related)
- Vehicle expenses (business-use percentage)
- Home office (if applicable -- square footage percentage)

### Special Deductions
- **Section 179:** Deduct full cost of equipment/assets up to $1,160,000 (2023) in the year purchased
- **QBI Deduction:** Qualified Business Income deduction -- 20% of qualified business income (subject to income limits and business type)
- **Health Insurance:** Self-employed health insurance premiums are deductible

## S-Corp Election Strategy

Once net profit exceeds approximately **$50,000-$80,000/year**, consider electing S-Corp tax status (Form 2553). This can significantly reduce self-employment tax.

### How It Works
- As a default LLC: All profit is subject to self-employment tax (15.3%)
- As an S-Corp: You pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll tax), and remaining profit is taken as distributions (NOT subject to self-employment tax)

### Example: $150,000 Net Profit

| | Default LLC | S-Corp Election |
|---|-----------|----------------|
| Net Profit | $150,000 | $150,000 |
| Reasonable Salary | N/A | $70,000 |
| Payroll Tax (15.3%) on salary | $22,950 (on all profit) | $10,710 (on salary only) |
| Distribution (no SE tax) | N/A | $80,000 |
| **SE Tax Savings** | | **~$12,240/year** |

**When to elect:** Consult a CPA. Generally beneficial when consistent net profit exceeds $50K+.

## Year-End Tax Planning Checklist (November - December)

- [ ] Review year-to-date income and expenses
- [ ] Estimate total annual profit
- [ ] Calculate estimated tax liability
- [ ] Make final quarterly estimated payment adjustment
- [ ] Accelerate deductible expenses into current year if beneficial
- [ ] Defer income to next year if in higher tax bracket
- [ ] Purchase needed equipment before Dec 31 (Section 179)
- [ ] Max out retirement contributions (SEP-IRA up to 25% of net income)
- [ ] Review health insurance deductions
- [ ] Organize all receipts and documentation
- [ ] Schedule meeting with CPA for tax filing preparation
- [ ] Evaluate S-Corp election for next year
- [ ] File 1099s for contractors paid $600+ (due Jan 31)
