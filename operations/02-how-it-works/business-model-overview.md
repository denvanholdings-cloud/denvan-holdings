# Business Model Overview

## How Denvan Holdings Works

---

## What Is Denvan Holdings?

Denvan Holdings LLC is a **parent holding company** that owns and manages multiple businesses (subsidiaries) across diverse industries. The holding company doesn't sell products or services directly to consumers -- instead, it provides the corporate infrastructure, shared services, and strategic management that each subsidiary needs to succeed.

## How Money Flows

### Step 1: Subsidiaries Earn Revenue
Each subsidiary operates its own business -- serving customers, selling products/services, and generating revenue in its specific industry.

### Step 2: Management Fee to Parent
Each subsidiary pays a **management fee** (10-15% of gross revenue) to Denvan Holdings for the shared services and corporate support it receives.

### Step 3: Parent Covers Shared Costs
Denvan Holdings uses the management fees to fund shared services: accounting, legal, HR, IT, marketing, and strategic planning that benefit all subsidiaries.

### Step 4: Profit Flows Up
After subsidiary operating expenses and management fees, remaining profit flows to the parent company as distributions.

### Step 5: Owner Distribution
The parent company distributes remaining profit to members after covering corporate overhead and maintaining reserves.

## The DBA Model

Each subsidiary operates as a **DBA (Doing Business As)** under Denvan Holdings LLC. This means:

- **One legal entity** (Denvan Holdings LLC) with multiple business names
- Each DBA is registered with the county clerk
- Subsidiaries share the parent's EIN, but can have sub-accounts
- Lower cost and simpler than forming separate LLCs for each business
- Can convert high-performing DBAs to separate LLCs later if needed

### DBA vs. Separate LLC

| Factor | DBA | Separate LLC |
|--------|-----|-------------|
| Setup cost | $25-50 | $300+ |
| Setup time | Days | Weeks |
| Liability separation | Shared | Protected |
| Tax filing | One return | Separate returns |
| Banking | Sub-accounts | Separate accounts |
| Best for | New/small subsidiaries | Large/high-risk businesses |

## Why This Model Works

### 1. Economies of Scale
Instead of each business paying for its own accountant, lawyer, HR person, and IT support, all subsidiaries share these services. A $500K subsidiary couldn't afford a CFO -- but it gets CFO-level financial management through the parent company.

### 2. Risk Diversification
If one subsidiary struggles, others continue generating revenue. The portfolio approach protects the overall business from single-industry downturns.

### 3. Faster Launches
New businesses start with infrastructure already in place -- accounting systems, legal framework, marketing support, and operational SOPs. Time to market is dramatically reduced.

### 4. Cross-Subsidiary Synergy
Businesses in the portfolio can refer customers to each other, share vendor relationships, and collaborate on combined offerings.

### 5. Scalable by Design
Adding a new business is straightforward: register a DBA, set up a sub-account, plug into shared services, and launch. The marginal cost of each new subsidiary decreases as the portfolio grows.

## Real-World Analogy

**Think of a shopping mall:**
- The mall (Denvan Holdings) owns the building, provides security, handles maintenance, runs marketing, and collects rent
- Each store (subsidiary) runs its own business, serves its own customers, and pays rent/fees to the mall
- Every store benefits from foot traffic generated by other stores
- The mall doesn't sell shoes or food -- it creates the environment where stores thrive

Denvan Holdings is the mall. The subsidiaries are the stores.
